Psycho-Cybernetics

Author: Maxwell Maltz

Core Idea

The central claim of Psycho-Cybernetics is:

Your subconscious mind works like a goal-seeking machine. The "self-image" you hold determines the results you consistently get in life.

According to Maltz:

  • You don't perform according to your potential.
  • You perform according to who you believe you are.

If you see yourself as:

  • lazy → you'll unconsciously act lazily.
  • confident → you'll naturally behave confidently.
  • successful → you'll make decisions that fit that identity.

Changing behavior without changing self-image usually fails.

Why Maltz believed this

Maltz was a plastic surgeon.

He noticed something strange:

Some patients received dramatic facial reconstruction and immediately became happier, more confident and outgoing.

Others looked objectively better... yet still believed they were ugly. Nothing changed psychologically.

He concluded:

The face wasn't the problem. The mental picture was.

Thus he developed Psycho-Cybernetics.

The Self-Image

The self-image is your internal definition of yourself.

Examples:

  • "I'm bad at math."
  • "I'm shy."
  • "I'm always late."
  • "I never finish projects."
  • "I'm disciplined."
  • "I'm the kind of person who exercises."

Your brain constantly tries to remain consistent with these beliefs. This is why changing habits is difficult.

If your self-image says: "I'm a procrastinator," then every productive day feels "unnatural." Eventually you relapse.

The Cybernetic Mechanism

Maltz compares the brain to a guided missile.

A missile:

  • Sets a destination.
  • Launches.
  • Goes off course.
  • Corrects.
  • Goes off course again.
  • Corrects again.
  • Eventually arrives.

Success works exactly the same.

People think successful people never fail and never make mistakes. Actually, they constantly correct. Failure is simply information.

Success Mechanism vs Failure Mechanism

Every person possesses two mental systems.

Success Mechanism

Activated by:

  • confidence
  • clear goals
  • positive expectation
  • learning
  • action

Produces:

  • creativity
  • persistence
  • calm
  • solutions

Failure Mechanism

Activated by:

  • fear
  • shame
  • anxiety
  • self-condemnation
  • worrying

Produces:

  • procrastination
  • hesitation
  • avoidance
  • excuses
  • quitting
Fear blocks the success mechanism.

Imagination Beats Willpower

One of Maltz's strongest claims: When imagination and willpower conflict... Imagination wins.

Example:
A person standing on a 30 cm wide board on the ground walks across easily.
Place that same board between two skyscrapers. Physically identical. Now they cannot.

Why? Not because of ability. Because imagination created fear.

Therefore: Changing imagination changes performance.

Mental Rehearsal

One of the book's major techniques. Athletes naturally visualize.

Maltz recommends mentally rehearsing success before acting.

Example:
Instead of imagining: "I'll embarrass myself."
Imagine: Walking calmly. Speaking clearly. Feeling confident. Leaving satisfied.

The subconscious treats vivid imagination similarly to repeated experience.

Relaxation

The subconscious performs best when relaxed. Overthinking interferes.

Examples:

  • Trying too hard to remember someone's name.
  • Trying too hard to sleep.
  • Trying too hard to be confident.

Effort often blocks performance. Instead: Relax. Trust the automatic mechanism.

The Servo-Mechanism

Your subconscious is like an automatic guidance system. It requires:

1. A target

Without goals, the mechanism wanders.

Examples:

  • "I want a programming job."
  • "I want to pass this exam."
  • "I want $10,000/month."

Goals give direction.

2. Information

You feed it: studying, practicing, experience.

The subconscious connects information later. This is why solutions often appear while showering, walking, or waking up.

3. Action

Thinking alone accomplishes nothing. Action gives feedback. Feedback allows correction.

Failure Is Data

One of the book's healthiest ideas: Failure isn't proof you're inadequate. It's navigation.

Like GPS: Wrong turn. Recalculate. Continue. That's all.

Emotionally attaching identity to failure damages the self-image.

Remove Emotional Scars

Past failures become dangerous when repeatedly replayed.

Instead of remembering: "I failed." Remember: "I learned."

Don't rehearse humiliation. Rehearse success.

Stop Self-Condemnation

Many people constantly criticize themselves: "I'm useless," "I'm behind," "I'm stupid."

Maltz argues this trains the subconscious to keep producing evidence supporting those statements.

Instead, separate:

"I made a mistake."

from

"I am a failure."

Huge difference.

The Importance of Goals

The subconscious cannot pursue vague objectives.

Bad goal: "I want a better life."

Better: "I will study programming for 2 hours." / "I will solve 20 exam questions."

Specific goals activate the success mechanism.

Happiness

Maltz argues happiness is not the reward. It is fuel.

Happy people think better, solve problems faster, and persist longer.

Therefore: Don't postpone happiness until success. Allow yourself to enjoy progress.

The Success Type Personality

Maltz lists characteristics of successful people. He summarizes them with the acronym SUCCESS:

S — Sense of Direction

Know where you're going.

U — Understanding

Accept reality. Learn continuously.

C — Courage

Act despite fear.

C — Charity

Treat others with goodwill. Avoid resentment.

E — Esteem

Respect yourself. Healthy self-esteem improves performance.

S — Self-Confidence

Built through repeated successful experiences. Not positive thinking alone.

S — Self-Acceptance

Accept yourself as a person while continuing to improve.

The Failure Personality

The opposite mindset includes:

  • fear
  • frustration
  • insecurity
  • loneliness
  • resentment
  • emptiness

These reinforce one another.

Practical Exercises

Maltz recommends:

1. Visualize daily

Spend several minutes imagining yourself succeeding.

2. Relax

Take time to reduce mental tension before important tasks.

3. Set goals

Give your subconscious a destination.

4. Practice success

Collect small wins. Confidence grows from evidence.

5. Stop replaying failures

Replay successful moments instead.

6. Act

Confidence follows action more often than it precedes it.

7. Speak kindly to yourself

Your internal dialogue shapes your self-image over time.

Main Lessons

  • Your self-image largely determines your behavior.
  • The subconscious is a goal-seeking system that works best with clear objectives.
  • Failure is feedback, not identity.
  • Mental rehearsal can improve real-world performance.
  • Relaxation helps the brain solve problems more effectively.
  • Imagination often has more influence over behavior than willpower.
  • Confidence grows through repeated action and successful experiences.
  • Lasting behavior change is easier when you first change how you see yourself.
